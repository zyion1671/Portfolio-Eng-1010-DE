DIAGNOSTIC ESSAY: ROOTS OF THE DAYCARE

Throughout majority of my life and the life of my parents and their siblings, my grandparents have had a building on their property in Arkansas. That building was originally a daycare center. My grandmother had run it even prior to any of her children being born, starting the daycare roughly around 1955. She started it to help make money alongside my grandpa who had been a worker laying down railroads at the time. The daycare thrived due to it being in a rural area with a tight-knit community. People often referred others to the daycare since they knew their kids would be well taken care of.

However, not long after my father's birth in 1985, my grandfather had become very close with the dean of the University of Arkansas at Pine Bluff. Due to that friendship and my grandfather's experience with manual labor and farming, he got offered an opportunity to teach at the university. Since my grandfather now had a larger salary, my grandmother felt she no longer needed to run the daycare. This led to some discourse between the older siblings and the younger since my grandmother's older children felt that the younger ones were spoiled since they didn't have to work in a daycare with screaming kids.

The daycare center officially became the designated storage area for any random bits and bobs on the property. As my grandparents got older, my grandfather started to hoard more stuff into the daycare and take less care of the building. The once organized and pristine daycare had now become a building with rotted wood and broken windows. Recently on a trip to Arkansas, we helped with the clearing and demolition of the daycare building. A month after the clearing, my grandmother told us that she had planted an herb garden in the same place. Everybody approved of it; they felt it should be something left on the property to remember the good impact the daycare had on our family.

LITERARY ANALYSIS: "THE LAST QUESTION"

In "The Last Question," Isaac Asimov uses repetition, symbolism, and a structure across vast periods of time to show that technological progress cannot remove humanity's instinct to question existence. At the beginning of the story, the question humans first ask the Multivac is, "How can the net amount of entropy of the universe be massively decreased?" (Asimov 181). Although the question seems scientific, it shows how humans can worry about the future even in ordinary moments.

As time passes and humans evolve, the same question continues to appear in different forms. Each time the question is asked, the computer responds with, "THERE IS INSUFFICIENT DATA FOR A MEANINGFUL ANSWER" (Asimov 186). This pattern reinforces the idea that despite progress, certain questions will still always be unanswered. The computer represents humanity's desire for certainty and control over the universe. As humans depend on the computer, it becomes a symbol of hope. At the same time, the computer's failure to answer shows the limits of knowledge. The computer grows more complex, changing from Multivac to Galactic AC and then Cosmic AC.

The structure of "The Last Question" moves across billions of years. Humanity evolves from physical bodies into pure energy, yet human nature remains constant. When humans vanish, Cosmic AC continues the search for answers. After the last stars fade, Cosmic AC discovers the answer to entropy. The machine then states, "LET THERE BE LIGHT" (Asimov 190). This line echoes the Bible, connecting the computer with the power of creation. Instead of ending the universe with silence, the story ends with a new beginning.

MOVIE REVIEW: ALTERED CARBON (EXACT SOURCE MANIFEST)

What happens when a writer creates a show that relates to real-world problems but uses a futuristic setting? How do audiences react? This was the thought process of Laeta Kalogridis, the writer of Netflix’s Altered Carbon. Altered Carbon is a science fiction show unlike any other, following the life of Takeshi Kovacs after being “re-sleeved” and employed by a “meth” to investigate his death. Takeshi lives in Bay City, a futuristic city fueled by inequality, wealth disparities, and corruption. Laeta and other crew members of Altered Carbon use Bay City along with a dark visual style and the growth of Takeshi to explore identity while also addressing real world issues. Through its atmosphere, narrative, and character development, Altered Carbon shows that identity becomes unstable when it is shaped and controlled by outside forces, ultimately reflecting real-world concerns about power and technology.

The atmosphere of Altered Carbon is meant to feel dark and controlled. Directors and production designers made deliberate choices to shape the environment. In an interview, Carey Meyer states, “You’re inside. You’re completely controlling the weather. You’re completely controlling the night and day issues” (Kiril). The control reflects the artificial world of the series. The show often takes place at night, which reinforces its tone. To emphasize this tone and its futuristic setting, Altered Carbon uses neon lights. Neon lights have been used in cyberpunk settings since Blade Runner. This visual reference connects Altered Carbon to earlier science fiction works, showing how the show builds on established ideas. The combination of darkness and neon lights creates a contrast that highlights the division in Bay City. The bright lights draw attention away from the decay underneath, similar to how the technology masks the corruption in society. The visual layout creates a feeling of restriction. The high-angle shots make the characters look small, which reinforces the theme of a powerful system controlling individuals.

The narrative of Altered Carbon focuses on how technology changes human identity, especially through the introduction of stacks and sleeves. Stacks are devices that hold human consciousness, while sleeves are the physical bodies. This division alters the way people view life and death, turning the body into an object. One of the first lines we hear in the series is, “Your body is not who you are. You shed it like a snake sheds its skin” (Kalogridis). This idea forces the audience to question what makes someone human. When memory can be transferred, the stability of individual identity is threatened. The narrative follows Takeshi as he faces problems moving between sleeves while trying to hold onto his identity. This conflict shows how identity can become unstable in a world built on control and technology. When identity can be controlled, it becomes a commodity. This change shows the danger of a society where even consciousness can be bought and sold. The rich, known as "meths," can live forever by changing sleeves, while the poor are stuck in old or damaged bodies. The narrative shows that technology does not solve inequality but makes it worse by creating a deeper division between social classes.

Character development also plays an important role in exploring identity. Takeshi’s growth throughout the series shows how outside forces can shape a person. At the beginning of the show, Takeshi is depicted as an elite soldier who has lost his purpose. His identity has been broken by years of warfare and imprisonment. As he investigates the death of Laurens Bancroft, he is forced to confront his past and the choices that shaped him. This internal conflict is shown through his interactions with other characters. His relationship with Kristin Ortega helps him reconnect with his humanity. Ortega reminds him of the value of real connections in a world where everything can be replaced. This connection causes Takeshi to question the system he is working for, leading him to reclaim his identity. His growth shows that while identity can be destabilized by external control, it can also be reclaimed through personal choice and connection. The development of Takeshi highlights the resilience of human nature against technological control.

Critics have evaluated Altered Carbon’s approach to identity and its futuristic setting. While some praise its visuals, others find its narrative complicated. A review from Escape Velocity notes that while the visuals are strong, “The plot is perhaps a bit too complex for the show to fully capture” (Collection). However, the complexity of the plot reflects the unstable world it portrays, where identity is constantly shifting and controlled by external forces. The acting also strengthens the show, as characters must portray consistent identities across different bodies. This adds another layer to the theme of instability, showing that identity is more than just physical appearance.

Altered Carbon presents a future where control extends beyond governments or systems and reaches into the most personal aspect of human existence: identity itself. The series does not just imagine advanced technology; it examines the consequences of that technology when placed in a society already shaped by inequality. The visual design, character struggles, and narrative structure all point to the same idea—when identity can be controlled, it becomes unstable. The world of Bay City may be fictional, but the concerns it raises about power, technology, and what it means to be human feel immediate and real.

COMMON READ REVIEW 1: HOW STRUGGLES CAN SHAPE A PERSON

What is it that makes you who you are, is it your values or your status? Maguerite Sheffer argues that your challenges are what builds you. Sheffer tries to show this using the short story “Reentry.” Throughout, Sheffer uses analogies to connect people and challenges to clay pots being fired. One of these analogies is, “‘but you cannot. You try, try your best, to be sure a pot is sound, but eventually you’ve got to fire it. An unfired pot is not a pot at all’” (Sheffer 45). Pottery being fired is like people going through challenges. You can try as hard as you want to prepare for it, but ultimately you don’t truly know the outcome until it happens.

Sheffer’s connection is further strengthened through the characters. The main character Frankie is a teen who has faced challenges in her life. As a result of these challenges, Frankie has become easily moldable like clay. Her pottery teacher is a man named Gary. Through his words of encouragement and advice Gary molds Frankie, “Soon, Frankie began to feel Greg’s influence in other parts of her life, where he was not” (Sheffer 46). Grant has become the potter while Frankie is the clay.

By the end of the story, Frankie has been fully molded by Gary and her challenges. Now when Frankie is faced with a challenge, Frankie instead faces it head on. When Frankie’s crew is forced into making a tough decision, Frankie is the one who decides they should take a lander. Frankie has finally realized what her fear was and is able to overcome it, “Frankie signaled to the commander that she had something to say. ‘It’s natural to be afraid,’ she began” (Sheffer 47).

COMMON READ REVIEW 2: WHAT REMAINS OF THE SHIP

In “The Disgrace of the Commodore” from The Man in the Banana Trees, Margeurite Sheffer contrasts the Commodore’s belief in strict preservation with the way later generations transform what he leaves behind. Through the image of a ship and the wood taken from it, Sheffer suggests that the past does not survive by remaining unchanged. Instead, it continues through the ways people adapt and reshape it.

The Commodore views preservation as the key to maintaining legacy. He believes objects must remain protected so they can be passed down through generations. This belief appears when he explains, “‘If we are to have anything to pass down, it must be carefully preserved’” (Sheffer 122). The statement reveals his rigid understanding of tradition and memory. In his mind, the value of the past depends on keeping it intact. This belief reflects his connection to authority and control. However, it also shows his inability to accept that later generations might understand the past in a different way.

The destruction of the ship challenges this belief and introduces a different interpretation of legacy. When the Commodore hears the vessel breaking apart, the moment symbolizes the collapse of the legacy he tried to protect. The Commodore describes how “He hears wood splintering. His descendants are hacking the ship to pieces” (Sheffer 122). To the Commodore, this act represents disgrace and the loss of his legacy. However, the scene also reveals that legacy does not always survive through preservation.

The wood of the ship becomes material that can serve new purposes. Instead of protecting the vessel as a monument to the past, the next generation turns it into something useful. The transformation shows that legacy is not fixed or permanent. What one generation tries to preserve may be reshaped by the next. Through the symbolism of the ship and its wood, Sheffer suggests that the past continues through change rather than perfect preservation.

MIDTERM ADAPTIVE SHORT STORY: WHAT THE WOOD KEEPS

This is my short story “What the Wood Keeps,” I based it off my interpretation of Marguerite Sheffer’s short story “Reentry.” The wood symbolizes lasting change, with its cracks and marks representing what can’t be changed. It also represents people; they can be changed by the challenges they face but we aren’t broken by them. I directly took this from “Reentry” since I interpretated Sheffer’s use of clay as being to show how people are molded by their challenges. I also used an unreliable narrator to show how memory can be unclear, yet the effect can remain.

The wood was already stacked when I got there. I do not remember cutting it, but that doesn’t mean I did not. The pieces were uneven, some split clean and others torn apart like they had resisted. They sat in a pile behind the house, close enough to the tree line that the shadows reached them first.

I told myself I must have done it the week before. That seemed reasonable. I picked up one of the pieces and turned it over in my hands. The grain curved in strange directions, lines blending where they should run straight. There were marks along the surface, shallow at first, then deeper toward the center. Not cuts exactly. More like pressure that had stayed. I set it back down. It felt wrong to move it too much.

The real estate agent said the old house used to belong to someone who worked with boats. I do not know if that was true, maybe they just liked the wood. In the morning, something is always slightly off. A piece turned the wrong way. Another closer to the door than I remember. Not enough to prove anything. Just enough to feel like I missed something important. I stopped trying to keep track. It felt useless.

The long piece by the window has a crack running through it now. I do not remember seeing it before. It follows the grain, splitting it without breaking it apart. When I press against it, the wood holds. It does not give in completely. It just shifts enough to remind me that it could. I think that is what bothers me most. Not that it might break, but that it hasn’t. I sit with it sometimes, watching the light move across the surface. The lines catch differently depending on the angle, revealing marks I had not noticed before. Some shallow. Others are deep enough that they must have taken time to form. I try to remember making them. I can’t but they’re there. That must mean something.

The neighbors always say wood like this always comes from somewhere else. That it carries the shape of what it used to be, even after it has been cut down and split apart. I do not know if I believe them, but when I look at the pieces scattered through the house, I see the pattern repeating. The same bends. The sane fractures. The same places where something pressed too hard and left its mark. I run my hand along the crack again. It has not spread. Not yet at least. The wood is still holding together. So am I. As far as I can tell.

MY FAVORITE STORY: MEMORY MARKET (UNABRIDGED)

The first memory I sold was small enough that I thought it wouldn’t matter. It was the smell of my grandmother’s kitchen, burnt sugar and cinnamon. The kind that sticks to your clothes long after you left. The kind that made you feel like you belonged somewhere.

“Common sensory memory,” The clerk said, barely looking up. “Not rare, I can offer you 30 credits.”

Thirty credits wasn’t much. But it was enough for lunch for a week, maybe two if I stretched it. “Deal,” I said. The process was quick. A thin wire rested against my temple, cool and almost gentle. For a moment, the memory played clearly in my mind. My grandmother laughing, the oven door creaking open, the warm rush of air. Then it flickered, and it was gone. Just like that.

“You may experience minor disorientation,” the clerk added, already calling for the next person. “Next.”

I stepped outside into the noise of the city. Cars, voices, screens glowing everywhere. Everything felt normal. Too normal. I told myself it didn’t matter. It was just a smell.

The second memory was easier. A birthday party from when I was seven. I barely remembered it, just flashes of ballons and too-sweet cake. The clerk offered more this time

“Fifty credits,” she said. “Childhood events are more valuable if they include emotional markers.”

I hesitated. “Will I… notice if it’s gone?” She shrugged “Only if you’re looking for it.” That answer should have stopped me. It didn’t. The hum. The flicker. Gone. This time, I walked out feeling lighter. Not empty, just less crowded. Like clearing space in a room, you didn’t realize was messy. I bought better food that night, real food.

Weeks passed, memory became currency. People lined up outside the market every morning: students, workers, even older people who looked like they had nothing to sell except the past itself. I started choosing more carefully. A conversation I couldn’t quite remember, a face from middle school, the sound of rain against my window on a specific night. Each time I tell myself, you’re not losing who you are, just trimming the edges. Then I met him.

“You’ve been coming here a lot lately,” he said leaning against the wall outside the market.

I didn’t recognize him. Not really but something about him felt familiar in a way I couldn’t explain. “Yeah,” I said, “so what?” He smiled but it didn’t reach his eyes.

“You don't remember me, do you?”
